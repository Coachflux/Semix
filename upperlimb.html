Definition of Anatomical Spaces and Tunnels

Anatomical spaces are potential or real compartments in the body that allow passage of neurovascular structures between regions. Tunnels are narrow fibro-osseous or fascial canals that transmit nerves, vessels, and tendons ‚Äî these are high-yield sites for compression syndromes (entrapment neuropathies).

1. Triangular Interval (Lower Triangular Space / Triceps Hiatus)

Location: Posterior axillary region, immediately inferior to the axilla and teres major; passageway between anterior and posterior compartments of the arm, and between posterior arm and axilla.

Boundaries:

  • Superior: Inferior border of teres major
  • Lateral: Shaft of the humerus and lateral head of triceps brachii
  • Medial: Lateral border of the long head of triceps brachii

Contents: Radial nerve ‚Ä¢ Profunda brachii artery (deep brachial artery) and accompanying venae comitantes.

Clinical Significance: Site where radial nerve and profunda brachii artery enter the posterior arm (via radial groove). Injury/compression here ‚Üí radial nerve palsy (wrist drop) or vascular compromise in humeral fractures.

2. Triangular Space (Upper Triangular Space)

Location: In the axilla, connecting axilla to posterior scapular region.

Boundaries:

  • Superior: Inferior border of teres minor (or subscapularis anteriorly)
  • Inferior: Superior border of teres major
  • Lateral: Medial margin of the long head of triceps brachii

Contents: Circumflex scapular artery (branch of subscapular artery).

Clinical Significance: Passage for scapular circumflex vessels; very important in shoulder surgeries and scapular vascular supply.

3. Quadrangular Space (Quadrilateral Space)

Location: Posterior shoulder/axilla region.

Boundaries:

  • Superior: Teres minor
  • Inferior: Teres major
  • Medial: Long head of triceps brachii
  • Lateral: Surgical neck/shaft of humerus

Contents: Axillary nerve ‚Ä¢ Posterior humeral circumflex artery (and vein).

Clinical Significance: Compression ‚Üí quadrangular space syndrome (axillary nerve entrapment ‚Üí deltoid & teres minor weakness, lateral arm paresthesia). Common in shoulder dislocations or trauma.

4. Axilla Region

Location: Pyramidal space between upper arm and lateral chest wall; apex points superiorly toward cervicoaxillary canal.

Boundaries:

  • Apex: 1st rib, clavicle, superior scapula
  • Base: Skin and axillary fascia
  • Anterior wall: Pectoralis major/minor, subclavius
  • Posterior wall: Subscapularis, teres major/minor, latissimus dorsi
  • Medial wall: Serratus anterior and ribs
  • Lateral wall: Intertubercular groove of humerus

Contents: Axillary artery & vein ‚Ä¢ Brachial plexus cords & branches ‚Ä¢ Lymph nodes (5 groups) ‚Ä¢ Fat & loose connective tissue ‚Ä¢ Proximal biceps brachii & coracobrachialis.

Clinical Significance: Major lymph drainage site for breast & upper limb; axillary lymph node dissection in breast cancer; brachial plexus injuries; axillary artery aneurysms/thrombosis.

5. Cubital Fossa

Location: Triangular depression anterior to elbow.

Boundaries:

  • Superior (base): Imaginary line between medial and lateral epicondyles
  • Medial: Pronator teres
  • Lateral: Brachioradialis
  • Roof: Bicipital aponeurosis, fascia, skin
  • Floor: Brachialis and supinator

Contents (medial to lateral ‚Äì TAN): Tendon of biceps brachii ‚Ä¢ Brachial artery (bifurcates into radial & ulnar) ‚Ä¢ Median nerve (Radial nerve deeper/lateral) ‚Ä¢ Median cubital vein (common for venepuncture).

Clinical Significance: Site for blood pressure measurement, venepuncture, brachial pulse palpation.

6. Ulnar Tunnel (Guyon's Canal)

Location: Ulnar side of wrist, superficial to flexor retinaculum.

Boundaries:

  • Roof: Volar carpal ligament / pisohamate ligament
  • Floor: Flexor retinaculum and hypothenar muscles
  • Ulnar wall: Pisiform bone
  • Radial wall: Hook of hamate

Contents: Ulnar nerve (superficial & deep branches) ‚Ä¢ Ulnar artery & vein.

Clinical Significance: Compression ‚Üí Guyon's canal syndrome (ulnar nerve entrapment ‚Üí hypothenar weakness, sensory loss in little/ring fingers; dorsal branch spared unlike cubital tunnel syndrome).

7. Carpal Tunnel

Location: Osteofibrous tunnel at wrist.

Boundaries:

  • Floor & walls: Carpal bones (scaphoid, trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, hamate, triquetrum, pisiform)
  • Roof: Flexor retinaculum (transverse carpal ligament)

Contents (9 structures): Median nerve + 8 flexor tendons (FDS √ó4, FDP √ó4, FPL √ó1).

Clinical Significance: Compression ‚Üí Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (most common entrapment neuropathy) ‚Üí pain, paresthesia in thumb/index/middle/ring fingers, thenar weakness/atrophy.

  • Tinel‚Äôs sign: Percussion over median nerve ‚Üí tingling

8. Extensor Tendon Compartments

Location: 6 fibro-osseous tunnels on dorsal wrist formed by extensor retinaculum.

Compartments (radial to ulnar) & Common Pathology:

  • 1: APL + EPB ‚Üí De Quervain's tenosynovitis
  • 2: ECRL + ECRB ‚Üí Intersection syndrome
  • 3: EPL ‚Üí Drummer's wrist, rupture in distal radius fracture
  • 4: EDC + EIP ‚Üí Extensor tenosynovitis
  • 5: EDM ‚Üí Vaughan-Jackson syndrome (ulnar deviation)
  • 6: ECU ‚Üí Snapping ECU

Clinical Significance: Tenosynovitis, tendon ruptures; very important in rheumatoid arthritis and wrist trauma.

9. Anatomical Snuffbox

Location: Triangular depression on radial/dorsal wrist, visible when thumb is abducted & extended.

Boundaries:

  • Medial: Tendon of extensor pollicis longus (EPL)
  • Lateral: Tendons of abductor pollicis longus (APL) + extensor pollicis brevis (EPB)
  • Floor: Scaphoid & trapezium bones
  • Roof: Skin & fascia

Contents: Radial artery (palpable pulse) ‚Ä¢ Cephalic vein ‚Ä¢ Superficial branch of radial nerve.

Clinical Significance: Tenderness ‚Üí scaphoid fracture (high risk of avascular necrosis). Also used for radial pulse palpation.
